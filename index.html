The Laziness Lie

Why "just try harder" isn't the problem — and what actually is

Welcome

8 to 10 minAbout today's group

Today is about one of the most damaging things neurodivergent people are told: that the reason things are hard is because they aren't trying hard enough.

We're going to look at where that story comes from, what it actually costs, and what a more honest explanation looks like. Not to make excuses. To get clearer.

The Laziness Lie

15 to 20 minWhere the story comes from

Dr. Devon Price's book Laziness Does Not Exist names a specific cultural myth — one that's especially destructive for neurodivergent people. It has three tenets.

01
Worth = Output
02
Never Enough
03
Don't Trust Yourself

Your worth is your productivity. The laziness lie starts here. What you produce, accomplish, and achieve is what makes you valuable — as an employee, a family member, a person. This is so embedded in American culture it often doesn't feel like a belief. It just feels like reality.

For neurodivergent people, whose output often doesn't look like neurotypical output, this tenet does particular damage. Variable productivity, non-linear work styles, or needing more time get read as moral failures.

There is always more you could be doing. Rest, downtime, doing nothing — these are not legitimate. Any time you're not producing, you're falling behind. Relaxing becomes a thing you have to earn. Being "busy" becomes an identity.

Research shows the average worker can genuinely focus for about three to four hours per day. The rest is relationship-building, reorganizing, and recovery. But the laziness myth demands eight hours of sustained output and calls anything less a character flaw.

If you're resting, you're wasting time. If you're tired, you should push through. If you need a break, you're weak.

You cannot trust your own needs and limitations. Feelings of exhaustion, overwhelm, or inability to start are not real data — they're excuses. Your body and nervous system are wrong. Push harder. This is the most insidious tenet because it severs the most important feedback loop you have.

For neurodivergent people with sensory differences, dysregulated nervous systems, or bodies that communicate differently, being taught to distrust internal signals creates serious long-term harm.

"You're not really tired." "Other people manage fine." "You're making excuses." "You just don't want to."
Price's core argument

The barrier question

If a person cares about getting something done yet repeatedly fails to do it, there are clearly barriers in their way. What they need is support removing those barriers — not shame about character flaws.

Price's rule: if someone can't do something, assume there's a reason. Not a moral reason. A real one. Exhaustion, overwhelm, unclear steps, anxiety, sensory load, or an environment that was never designed for how their brain works.

This isn't a soft reframe. It's a different explanatory model entirely. Situational constraints predict behavior far better than personality or willpower — but the laziness myth keeps the focus on the person rather than the context.

The shame loop

How difference becomes dysfunction

Neurodivergent people don't start with dysfunction. They start with difference. But the shame about that difference creates dysfunction — which produces more shame, which creates more dysfunction. Then they get blamed for the very thing the shame created.

What's labeled
  • Lazy
  • Unmotivated
  • Not trying
  • Making excuses
  • Irresponsible
  • Difficult
What's actually happening
  • Executive dysfunction
  • Dopamine deficit, task paralysis
  • Barriers that aren't visible
  • Real limitations being named
  • Nervous system dysregulation
  • Sensory overload or burnout

KC Davis: as a therapist, she's never gotten to the bottom of why someone struggles with something and found it was simply because they were lazy. There is almost always a legitimate barrier — pain, fatigue, executive dysfunction, overwhelm, burnout, lack of support, or mental health.

Executive Function

15 to 20 minWhat's actually going on in your brain

Executive dysfunction — your brain not doing what you tell it to do — is one of the most misunderstood aspects of neurodivergent experience. It's not a willpower failure. It's a neurological one.

What executive function actually is
+

Executive function covers the mental processes that help you plan, initiate, sequence, and complete tasks. This includes starting things, switching between things, regulating attention, managing time, and tolerating the discomfort of boring or hard work.

Price notes that the very concept is culturally loaded: executive functioning essentially demands that each one of us be a high-powered corporate executive managing the business that is our own lives — setting agendas, tracking schedules, sequencing all steps toward goals while maintaining home, family, and self.

For brains wired differently, this framework was never a good fit. The demand is neurotypical by design.
Dopamine and the motivation system
+

Executive dysfunction has a lot to do with dopamine — the brain's incentive chemical. For many neurodivergent brains, especially ADHD, dopamine doesn't release on cues that work for neurotypical brains. Tasks aren't automatically "tagged" as worth starting.

This isn't a choice. You can't willpower your way to dopamine. The brain that "just can't start" isn't being defiant — it literally doesn't have the neurochemical signal that says this matters, go now.

Interest, urgency, challenge, and novelty tend to create dopamine spikes. Routine, obligation, and abstract future reward often don't — which explains a lot about why certain tasks stay undone.
Procrastination reframed
+

Price makes an important point about procrastination: it's more likely when a task is meaningful and the person cares about doing it well. When someone fails to begin, it's typically because of anxiety about not being good enough, or genuine confusion about what the first steps are.

Procrastination is not a character trait. It's a response to a specific set of conditions — usually some combination of unclear initiation point, fear of imperfection, overwhelm, or a mismatch between the task's demands and how the brain processes them.

If you care deeply about something and still can't start — that's not laziness. That's often the highest form of it.
Body doubling and external structure
+

Body doubling — having another person present while you work — is one of the most effective tools for neurodivergent executive dysfunction. Price notes that while clinicians prescribe stimulant medication, no one has systematically studied prescribing a body double, despite it often addressing the underlying problem more directly.

ADHD and autism are often diagnosed as individual deficits. But many symptoms are situational. People with ADHD are highly socially motivated — in social contexts, they often function significantly better. It's worth asking why we pathologize the need for others at all, when nearly every human being is a social animal.

External scaffolding — another person, a timer, a change of environment, a structured routine — isn't a crutch. It's an accommodation. The same way glasses aren't a crutch for eyes.
Rest as necessity, not reward
+

The laziness lie treats rest as something you earn. Price argues the opposite: rest is biological necessity. Not just from physical labor, but from sustained cognitive load, sensory input, and social performance.

For neurodivergent people who are masking, code-switching, or navigating environments not designed for them, the rest needed to recover is significantly higher than what the laziness myth allows. Needing more recovery time isn't weakness. It's an accurate reading of a higher metabolic cost.

Quiet downtime — the kind where you're not consuming or producing anything — is where the nervous system restores itself. It is not wasted time.
Discussion

20 to 25 minOpen it up

These are starting points. Take them wherever feels real. There are no wrong answers here.

Opening
Which of the three tenets — worth equals output, never enough, don't trust yourself — has done the most damage for you personally?
The barrier question
Think of something you've been calling laziness or failure in yourself. What would a barrier-focused explanation look like instead of a character-focused one?
Give people time here. This one can be hard to land.
Executive function
When does executive dysfunction show up most for you? What conditions make it better or worse — and which of those conditions are you actually in control of?
The shame loop
What's the story you tell yourself when you can't do something you think you should be able to do? Where did that story come from?
Closing round
What's one accommodation, structure, or condition that genuinely helps you function better — that you've been reluctant to use or ask for because it felt like cheating?

Let the conversation find its own pace. Some questions will land harder than others. That's the work.

Facilitator note

The goal today wasn't to give people an excuse not to try. It was to replace a false explanation with a true one. When people understand what's actually happening, they can actually address it. Shame just keeps the loop running.
